Part 1

Biological Origins — A Tree, Not a Ladder

Two ancestral populations

A 2025 Cambridge study (Nature Genetics) found Homo sapiens emerged from two populations separated for over 1.5 million years. Roughly 20% of modern human DNA derives from "Population B" — genes concentrated in regions linked to brain function.

Southern Africa's isolated branch

The largest ancient African DNA study to date (Uppsala University, Nature, 2025) found southern Africans lived in genetic isolation for 200,000 years. Over 40% of their unique DNA variants are associated with neurons and brain growth.

Lineages older than thought

A reconstructed 1-million-year-old skull (Yunxian 2, Science, 2025) shows all five major human lineages diverged more than a million years ago — roughly double previous estimates.

Part 2

Cognitive Evolution — When Minds Became Modern

Homo sapiens had a modern brain architecture by ~300,000 years ago, yet complex culture only persists in the archaeological record from ~75,000–40,000 years ago. Behavioural modernity is not a single genetic event — it is an emergent property requiring neural substrate, population density, and robust social networks.

Symbolic language

Complex, recursive communication enabling abstract and displaced thought.

Cumulative culture

Each generation builds on the last rather than reinventing from scratch.

Theory of mind

Modelling other minds — the foundation of large-scale cooperation.

Symbolic art

Ochre engravings at Blombos Cave (~100,000 ya); shell jewellery (~160,000 ya).

Shared intentionality

Uniquely human "we"-thinking — the critical prerequisite for language, culture, and institutions.

Part 3

Cultural & Civilisational Evolution

From mobile foragers to networked global civilisation — 12,000 years of cultural acceleration in six pivotal steps.

1

Hunter-Gatherer Era (~300 kya – 12 kya)

Small egalitarian bands; deep ecological knowledge; sophisticated oral culture.

2

Agricultural Revolution (~12 kya)

Surplus, sedentism, and population growth; reshapes bodies, hierarchy, and psychology.

3

Urban Revolution (~5 kya)

Writing, law, specialisation, and long-distance trade emerge in Mesopotamia and beyond.

4

Axial Age (~800–200 BCE)

Independent flowering of philosophy, ethics, and religion across Eurasia.

5

Scientific & Industrial Revolution (~1500–1900)

Empiricism, exponential energy use, mass literacy, and global economic integration.

6

Information & AI Revolution (~1950–present)

Externalisation of cognition; collective intelligence networked across billions of minds.

Part 4

The Future of Human Evolution

Three possible trajectories are unfolding in parallel: biological change, biological design, and technological augmentation.

Biological evolution

Slow, natural selection continues, but new pressures from medicine and migration are changing the environment in which it operates.

Genetic & biotechnological evolution

CRISPR and other biotechnologies shift evolution from a natural process toward conscious selection and design.

Technological augmentation

Brain-computer interfaces, neural implants, life extension, and AI-human integration point toward a contested post-biological future.

"The idea of species evolving in clean, distinct lineages is too simplistic."

— Trevor Cousins, Cambridge, 2025
